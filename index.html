Cholera is an infectious disease that has claimed the lives of tens of millions of humans since the 19th century. Outbreaks of Cholera are particularly grim — victims die of dehydration but blood continues to flow to their brains. They remain conscious as their organs begin to fail, fully aware of their slow death. Whole families would perish together in this way over several days.

While cholera outbreaks continue to happen (the latest incidence is April 2017 in Yemen at the time of writing), for most countries, they are a thing of the past. We ended this epidemic not by the discovery of new medicine (cholera has a very simple cure: drink lots of clean water) but by understanding its transmission.

Enacting public health policies without understanding how diseases spread could very well make things worse and cost lives. In fact, this is exactly what happened in the 1854 cholera outbreak in London. It was believed that the unsanitary conditions of cesspools around the city were poisoning the air and spreading the disease. The Sewer Commission launched an elaborate campaign to clean up the city — enforcing by law that raw sewage contained in houses be dumped into the Thames river. As a result, the city smelled much nicer, but Cholera is a water-borne disease. Drinking infected water is how you contract it. A violent outbreak struck soon after.

Our Goal

We want to understand how cholera spreads so that we can predict and prevent any outbreaks. The introduction of mathematical models to the field of epidemiology is what allows us to do this.

The people who decided to dump the sewage into the river were not dim. There was a lot of evidence that seemed to suggest that the filthy air was the cause. It made sense intuitively.

Mathematical models allow us to gain insight into complex mechanisms, exactly when human intuition fails. We're going to look at how these mathematical tools can tame this complexity and allow us to make informed decisions.
